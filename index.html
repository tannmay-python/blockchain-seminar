Definition

What is a blockchain?

A blockchain is a distributed ledger — a database shared and synchronized across many computers, with no central authority. Records are grouped into blocks, each carrying a cryptographic hash of the previous block. That chaining makes the past tamper-evident.

🌐

Distributed

No single point of failure or control. Every node keeps a copy.

🔒

Immutable

Altering one past block invalidates every block that follows it.

🔎

Transparent

Any participant can independently verify the entire ledger.

Cryptographic Foundations

The hash function — the atom of the chain

SHA-256 takes any input and returns a fixed 256-bit fingerprint. It is deterministic, one-way, and exhibits the avalanche effect: flip a single character and roughly half the output bits change. Type below — this runs a real SHA-256 in your browser.

Data Structure

Why "chain"? Tamper with it and find out.

Each block contains data, its own hash, the previous block's hash, and a nonce. Edit any block's data below — its hash changes, which breaks its link to the next block, cascading down the chain. Then re-mine to find a nonce that satisfies the difficulty rule (hash must start with 0000).

Cryptographic Foundations

Who owns what: public-key signatures

Each participant holds a private key (secret) and a public key (shared). You sign a transaction with the private key; anyone verifies it with the public key — without ever learning your secret. This is the basis of wallet ownership. Try signing, then tamper with the message.

Cryptographic Foundations

Merkle trees — verify one transaction, trust the whole block

Transactions are hashed pairwise up a tree to a single Merkle root. To prove a transaction is in a block, you don't need the whole block — only a handful of sibling hashes along the path to the root. Click any transaction to see its Merkle proof.

Consensus Mechanisms

How strangers agree without a boss

Consensus is how nodes agree on the true state of the ledger with no central authority. The two dominant approaches make opposite bets on what is scarce: computation vs. capital.

Live · Proof of Work — the mining race

Four miners race to find a nonce whose block hash starts with the target zeros. More hash power = more guesses/sec = better odds. Winner takes the block.

Live · Proof of Stake — weighted lottery

No racing. A validator is chosen at random, weighted by stake. Adjust the stakes and run the lottery — 99% less energy. Ethereum switched in 2022 (The Merge).

PoW

Spend electricity to earn the right to write. Secure, battle-tested, energy-hungry. Bitcoin

PoS

Lock capital as collateral; misbehave and it's slashed. Ethereum

Other

Delegated PoS, Proof of Authority (enterprise), BFT (Tendermint, PBFT). Each trades off speed, decentralization, security.

Programmability

The frontier: privacy, usability, real assets, and connecting the islands.

Frontier

Zero-Knowledge Proofs

Prove you know something without revealing it. The classic intuition: the Ali Baba cave. Peggy claims she knows the secret word that opens a door connecting two tunnels. She can prove it to skeptical Victor — without ever saying the word. Run the rounds: each one halves the chance a liar gets lucky.

Real-World Asset Tokenization

Representing ownership of a real asset as an on-chain token. T-bills, private credit, real estate, trade-finance receivables. Why? 24/7 settlement, fractional ownership, programmable compliance, less counterparty risk.

Frontier

CBDCs — state-issued digital currency

A central bank's own digital money. Crucially not a cryptocurrency: it's centralized, state-controlled, and permissioned. Crypto and CBDCs sit at opposite ends of the control spectrum.

Crypto ↔ CBDC — the control spectrum

Key debate: programmable money that can expire or be restricted raises civil-liberties concerns; bank disintermediation risk; privacy of state-visible transactions.

Frontier

Interoperability — connecting the islands

Hundreds of chains, assets siloed. Bridges lock assets on one chain and mint representations on another — and have been crypto's single largest attack surface: over $2.5B lost to bridge hacks (Ronin, Wormhole, Nomad) in 2021–2023.
